Research Proposal · Direction Selection

Efficient Hybrid Retrieval for Granite RAG: How Cheap Can “Good Enough” Get?

Can a cheap hybrid — a small Granite embedding fused with BM25 — buy big-model retrieval quality at a fraction of the cost?

Project: IBM Granite “Needle in a Haystack” / Enterprise Retrieval Partners: University of Bristol × IBM (Bharat Arora) Submission: 2026-09-04

1.Summary

The Granite retrieve-then-generate system is the project’s main line and its RAG MVP is already working. For the deeper research deep-dive, the supervisor asked us to go deep on one or two directions. After comparing all four, we merge hybrid retrieval and efficiency (cost–quality) into a single question:

Core question
For Granite RAG, where is the cost–quality sweet spot for retrieval, and can a cheap hybrid (a small Granite embedding fused with BM25) recover the quality of an expensive dense retriever at a fraction of the cost?

The contribution is a cost–quality frontier for Granite RAG retrieval, with hybrid fusion studied as an efficiency lever, all measured with a downstream-grounded evaluation. It is a rigorous, deployable, low-risk applied contribution; methodological novelty (cheap attribution) is kept as upside, not the bet.

2.Motivation

Enterprises embed and search very large, noisy document collections, then generate answers grounded in the retrieved evidence. At that scale, cost matters as much as accuracy: embedding millions of documents, serving low-latency retrieval, and running a generator all cost money and hardware. The interesting question for deployment is therefore not “is the biggest model best?” (it usually is) but “how cheap can we go and stay good enough?” — can a smaller, cheaper model do the job?

A second, complementary lever is fusion: a small dense model and a free lexical retriever (BM25) have complementary strengths, so cheaply combining them may recover the quality of a much larger model. This proposal studies exactly that trade-off.

3.What we already have (foundation)

This proposal builds on a working, tested base — it is not a fresh start:

  • First results in. On SciFact, Granite dense already beats both baselines: nDCG@10 = 0.767 (Granite) vs 0.636 (BM25) ≈ 0.641 (sentence-transformers).
  • A swappable evaluation harness. BM25 / sentence-transformers / Granite retrievers behave identically through one interface; an ablation layer already sweeps chunk size, pooling, and embedding model from the command line.
  • A verified RAG MVP. A retrieve→generate→cite demo, with the Granite generator verified on real models (3B locally, 8B on the university HPC).

So the assets needed for this direction — both retrievers, multiple model sizes, and the evaluation pipeline — already exist.

4.Directions considered (the supervisor’s four)

The supervisor named four candidate deep-dive directions: advanced prompting, hybrid retrieval, explainability, and efficiency. We assessed each on novelty ceiling, cost, scoop risk (how crowded the area is), and how much it reuses what we have built.

Decision. We pick hybrid retrieval and efficiency (cost–quality) — and unify them, because hybrid fusion is itself an efficiency lever (it lets cheap components punch above their weight). Advanced prompting is absorbed into ordinary RAG tuning rather than run as a separate study. Explainability has the highest novelty ceiling but our own literature review found the faithfulness / attribution space densely occupied (SABER, ProbeRAG, ALCE, Self-RAG…); we therefore keep it as research upside, consistent with our locked “floor on rigour, novelty as upside” plan.

5.Chosen direction & research questions

The unifying insight ties the two primary directions into one arc:

The arc
A small/cheap dense retriever loses to the big one on its own — but a cheap hybrid (small Granite embedding + BM25) may recover most of that loss at a fraction of the cost. We therefore map the cost–quality frontier and test whether hybrid moves it up and to the left (higher quality, lower cost).
  • RQ1 (the frontier). Across configurations — BM25 / small Granite embedding / full Granite embedding, × 3B / 8B generation, × context size (top-k) — what is the cost–quality frontier of Granite RAG, measured downstream?
  • RQ2 (hybrid as the lever). Where does the cheap dense retriever fail (entity / numeric / date / rare-term queries), and does a cheap RRF hybrid of small dense + BM25 recover those failures and approach full-Granite quality — without regressing the queries it already answered?
  • RQ3 (what transfers). Which gains in retrieval precision actually translate into better downstream answers, and which do not? (Following eRAG: shallow relevance labels correlate poorly with end-to-end RAG quality.)

6.Methodology & evaluation

The method is a controlled optimisation loop. Rigour comes from holding the evaluation fixed and changing one component at a time — not from a clever narrative.

Fix the yardstick  ->  Maximise (tune)  ->  Diagnose (failure analysis)
                            |                         |
                            v                         v
                   Augment (cheap hybrid)  ->  Verify (no-regression)
  • Fixed yardstick. Same datasets, same k, same harness throughout. Metrics: precision / recall / nDCG / MRR for retrieval; answer correctness + faithfulness for RAG.
  • Downstream-grounded evaluation (eRAG). Following Salemi & Zamani (SIGIR’24), each retrieved passage is judged by its effect on the LLM’s answer — the more faithful measure of “did we retrieve evidence that actually helps?”
  • Cost axes (defined up front). Embedding model size, index size, retrieval latency, and generation token cost — reported consistently so the frontier is honest.
  • Datasets. SciFact (done) + 2–3 further BEIR sets for robustness + Natural Questions (it carries both relevance labels and gold answers, so retrieval and RAG are evaluated on the same data).
  • Augmentation. A HybridRetriever using Reciprocal Rank Fusion (Cormack 2009) — rank-based, no score normalisation, parameter-light; it drops into the existing harness as one more row.
  • Verification rule. An improvement counts only if it raises overall quality, recovers the diagnosed failure subset, and does not regress the queries already answered well.
  • Compute. Retrieval and evaluation on CPU/laptops; Granite generation on the university HPC (already verified).
  • Small-data-first (de-risking). Per the supervisor’s guidance, every experiment is first run end-to-end on a small slice (e.g. ~100–200 Natural Questions queries) before scaling to the full datasets, so integration problems surface cheaply, before committing HPC time.

7.Expected outcome — the cost–quality frontier

The headline deliverable is one figure and one sentence: a cost–quality frontier with the cheap hybrid sitting in the upper-left sweet spot, plus a deployable claim of the form “small + BM25 reaches ~X% of full-Granite quality at ~Y% of the cost.”

Honest scoping
This is the safe-distinction play, not a swing for a novel method: the contribution is rigorous applied engineering + analysis, with a moderate (not high) novelty ceiling.
