Research Proposal

Granite-Powered Retrieval and RAG: A Faithful System with Rigorous, Downstream-Grounded Evaluation

A Granite RAG system is the project's main line; rigorous evaluation, failure analysis, and cheap attribution are the research contributions layered on top.

Project: IBM Granite "Needle in a Haystack" / Enterprise Retrieval Partners: University of Bristol × IBM (Bharat Arora) Submission: 2026-09-04

1. Summary

We build a Granite-powered retrieval and RAG system that finds rare, relevant evidence in large corpora and generates answers that are grounded in — and cited to — that evidence. The system is the project's main line. On top of it, our research work consists of: (i) a rigorous, transparent comparison of Granite against standard baselines using a downstream-grounded evaluation; (ii) an analysis of where and why Granite fails; and (iii) a deeper, optional study of cheap, embedding-based answer attribution. The rigour and analysis are research contributions layered on the system, not a replacement for it.

2. Motivation

Organisations must locate rare but critical evidence ("needles") inside large, noisy document collections — legal discovery, compliance, healthcare, finance. Even a strong language model produces a wrong answer when the retrieved passage is wrong, so both retrieval accuracy and answer faithfulness matter. IBM's open-source Granite embedding models provide a strong semantic retriever; this project measures how well they serve this need and delivers a usable RAG system built around them.

3. The System (project main line)

A modular retrieve-then-generate pipeline built on one shared retrieval core:

User query
  -> Retrieval (BM25 / sentence-transformers / Granite dense / hybrid)
  -> Top-k evidence passages
  -> Granite answer generation
  -> Answer + source citations
  • V1 — Granite dense retrieval (baseline system). Chunk the corpus, embed with Granite, index in FAISS, retrieve top-k by semantic similarity. (Built; first results in.)
  • V2 — Hybrid retrieval (engineering improvement). Fuse Granite dense with BM25 lexical retrieval (rank fusion) to recover exact matches — names, dates, numbers, rare terms — that dense retrieval misses. A standard, low-cost step given our architecture (BM25 and dense already share one interface); an engineering improvement, not a research bet.
  • RAG MVP + citation. Feed retrieved passages to the Granite generative model to produce answers, and attribute each answer to its supporting source so users can verify it.

Deliverable: a working, demonstrable Granite RAG system with source citations.

4. Research Contributions (layered on the system)

These are the research work we submit; they sit on top of the system.

C1 — Rigorous, transparent, downstream-grounded comparison

(Addresses the supervisor's "no vague claims".) We compare Granite dense against BM25 and a peer neural baseline (sentence-transformers) on standard IR metrics (precision/recall/nDCG/MRR) and on a downstream-grounded retrieval evaluation: following eRAG (Salemi & Zamani, SIGIR'24), the downstream LLM itself judges each retrieved document's usefulness, because plain query–document relevance labels correlate poorly with end-to-end RAG performance. We report across multiple datasets with explicit, verifiable links to published baseline numbers, and we clearly state dataset/version differences.

C2 — Granite failure-mode analysis

(Addresses the supervisor's "find the edge cases".) We characterise where Granite underperforms: per query, we find cases where BM25 beats Granite and categorise them (entity-heavy, numeric, date, rare-term, very short queries). Surfacing weaknesses demonstrates rigour and openness, and motivates the hybrid step in V2.

C3 — Cheap embedding-based attribution (research upside, not a dependency)

As a deeper, optional study: can we measure how strongly an answer is grounded in its retrieved sources using the retriever's own embeddings — a training-free attribution signal — and is it competitive with heavier alternatives? We further ask whether retrieval quality determines attributability. This extends eRAG's idea (whose downstream signal is accuracy) to faithfulness / attributability, and is distinct from corrective-RAG work (CRAG, Self-RAG) that trains a controller to resolve conflicts. We pursue C3 only if time allows; the project's success does not depend on it.

5. Research Questions

  • RQ1. How much do Granite dense and hybrid retrieval improve retrieval and end-to-end RAG quality over BM25 and sentence-transformers, measured with downstream-grounded metrics across datasets?
  • RQ2. On which query types does Granite fail, and why?
  • RQ3 (upside). Can cheap, embedding-based attribution faithfully measure answer grounding, and does retrieval quality predict attributability?

6. Methodology and Evaluation

  • Pipeline: chunk → embed (Granite / sentence-transformers) → FAISS index → retrieve (BM25 / ST / Granite / hybrid) → Granite generation → answer + citations.
  • Retrieval evaluation: standard IR metrics + eRAG-style downstream-grounded relevance.
  • RAG evaluation: answer correctness (vs gold) + faithfulness (RAGAS).
  • Datasets: SciFact (retrieval, done), additional BEIR sets (NFCorpus / FiQA / TREC-COVID) for robustness, and Natural Questions (qrels and gold answers, so retrieval and RAG are evaluated on the same data).
  • Failure analysis: per-query nDCG; categorise Granite-vs-BM25 losses.
  • Compute: retrieval and evaluation on CPU/laptops; Granite generation on the university HPC (already verified).

7. Positioning (how this differs from recent work)

The "reliable / abstaining / faithful RAG" space is active; we position our contribution against it deliberately.

Recent lineWhat it doesHow we differ
CRAG / Self-RAG / Adaptive-RAGTrain a corrective controller to assess retrieval and fix it (re-retrieve, filter, abstain).We do not train a controller or resolve conflicts; we deliver a Granite RAG system plus a rigorous evaluation and failure analysis.
eRAG (Salemi & Zamani, SIGIR'24)Evaluate retrieval quality by its downstream impact (accuracy).We use eRAG as a tool and extend its downstream signal from accuracy to faithfulness/attributability, applied to the Granite-vs-baseline question.
SABER / abstention workDecide when to answer vs abstain.We do not do abstention; cheap embedding attribution is our research angle.

8. Timeline and Feasibility

  • Now → early August: complete the multi-dataset rigorous comparison (C1), the failure analysis (C2), and the RAG MVP; pursue C3 as time allows.
  • Last ~3 weeks: report and presentation.

The scope is achievable because it reuses the existing, tested retrieval core and uses eRAG as an off-the-shelf evaluation tool. The only open-ended item (C3) is upside and can be dropped without affecting the core deliverable — keeping us on schedule before the team disperses in August.

9. Expected Outcome

A working, cited Granite RAG system; a rigorous, transparent, multi-dataset comparison showing where Granite helps and where it fails; and — time permitting — a cheap attribution study. A complete, defensible deliverable whose strength rests on engineering quality, evaluation rigour, and critical analysis, with the attribution study as additional research depth.
